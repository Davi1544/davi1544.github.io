## Studies

### Topic: t-SNE and PCA

Dimensionality reduction is the process of reducing the number of features describing data points whilst attempting to keep as much of the original information as possible. This is achieved by mapping those points to a lower-dimensional space. 

Out of the many techniques available, I studied t-SNE and PCA. t-SNE is non-deterministic and works well with non-linear data. PCA is deterministic and works best when the features of the inputs have linear relationships. Most importantly, t-SNE is designed to preserve local groups while PCA is designed to preserve global structures.

Although both algorithms do the same job, t-SNE seems to be the more promising of the two for the purposes of this project. Despite t-SNE's slower execution time, I expect it to perform better than PCA on palimpsests because it produces more separated clusters. This is useful because more separated clusters might yield better results after applying clustering algorithms. We'll discuss this further in the *Experiments* section. Finally, t-SNE's preservation of local groups might also be extremely useful when our goal is to find pixel patches that might contain hidden text.

## Experiments
To test t-SNE versus PCA, I used the [Kaggle Titanic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hobbit#Lifestyle) dataset. By reducing the number of dimensions from 8 to 2, my goal was to plot the final results and check if either algorithm managed to preserve survivors and non-survivors in separate groups.

As expected, t-SNE had a much better result, managing to keep both groups relatively separate.

![t-SNE Results]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/week1-TSNE-results.png)

![PCA Results]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/week1-PCA-results.png)

Observe that t-SNE managed to group most of the survivors in a compressed section, while PCA spread survivors and non-survivors across all major groupings.
