## Studies

### Topic: t-SNE and PCA

Dimensionality reduction is the process of reducing the number of features describing data points whilst attempting to keep as much of the original information as possible. This is achieved by mapping those points to a lower-dimensional space. 

Out of the many techniques available, I studied t-SNE and PCA. t-SNE is non-deterministic and works well with non-linear data. PCA is deterministic and works best when the features of the inputs have linear relationships. Most importantly, t-SNE is designed to preserve local groups while PCA is designed to preserve global structures.

Although both algorithms do the same job, t-SNE seems to be the more promising of the two for the purposes of this project. Despite t-SNE's slower execution time, I expect it to perform better than PCA on palimpsests because it produces more separated clusters. This is useful because more separated clusters might yield better results after applying clustering algorithms. We'll discuss this further in the *Experiments* section. Finally, t-SNE's preservation of local groups might also be extremely useful when our goal is to find pixel patches that might contain hidden text.

## Experiments
To test t-SNE versus PCA, I used the [Kaggle Titanic](https://www.kaggle.com/c/titanic) dataset. By applying both techniques to reduce the data from eight dimensions to two, my goal was to plot the final results and evaluate if either algorithm managed to preserve survivors and non-survivors in separate clusters.

As discussed before, t-SNE aims to preserve local groups, so I expected it to group survivors and non-survivors in separate clusters. PCA, on the other hand, aims to preserve global structures, so I expected it to do worse at preserving the distinctiveness of these groups.

The results of this experiment follow:

![t-SNE Results](/assets/img/posts/week1/week1-TSNE-results.png)

![PCA Results](/assets/img/posts/week1/week1-PCA-results.png)

Observe that t-SNE managed to group most of the survivors in an isolated region, while PCA spread survivors and non-survivors across all major groupings.

In the context of this project, we can assume all pixels of the images in our dataset somewhat fall into two groups: the ones that contain some hidden information and the ones that do not. As we'll discuss in detail in later posts, all images in our dataset have 16 channels, which we can interpret as features. Therefore, we can roughly interpret a multispectral image as another set of points with a high number of features, just like the Titanic dataset. For this reason, the results of this experiment make me optimistic about the use of t-SNE in future stages of the project.
