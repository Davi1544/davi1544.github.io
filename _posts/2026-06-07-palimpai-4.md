# Week 4 - Exploring the MISHA dataset

## Overview

Week 4 was spent exploring the MISHA dataset and finally conducting some exploratory experiments on it.

## Experiments

I am currently working with a sample of the MISHA dataset composed of 24 multispectral images of 12 cultural heritage works (front and back), which include documents and possible drafts of literary work (manuscripts and early works).

All sample images have 16 channels with each channel mapped to a target wavelength when the picture was taken. The lengths vary from 365 nms (in the ultraviolet range) to 940 nms (in the infrared range).

### t-SNE vs PCA

In the previous weeks, we've discussed dimensionality reduction techniques. I'd concluded earlier that t-SNE would probably perform better than PCA in the context of this project. However, all articles I read on the topics of this research project preferred to use PCA or ICA (or both), eventhough t-SNE was already available.

To test my hypothesis, I chose to apply both algorithms to a sample image and evaluate the results. Before we discuss the experiment, it's important to note that all articles I read on this topic applied some level of preprocessing to the target images before applying dimensionality reduction, so the results of this experiment were not meant to be decisive.

After applying PCA on the **raw** data of a subject image (whose RGB representation is the first image of the last section), this was the result:

![toRGB Results](/assets/img/posts/week4/pca_3_bands.png)

Observe that the underlying text is hardly any easier to read, which once again reiterates the importance of preprocessing these images, which will be done later on.

However, when I tried to apply t-SNE, I got an unexpected result: the computer ran out of memory. So, after spending some time digging on the reasons for this problem, I learned t-SNE does not scale well to high numbers of data points. In the context of our subject image, it contains more than 20 million pixels, making classic t-SNE inapplicable.

In conclusion, the results of applying PCA and failed application of t-SNE show why the previously discussed articles might've preferred PCA (with expressive previous preprocessing) over t-SNE, as the cost of applying the naive version of t-SNE might outweigh the potential improvement compared to PCA.
