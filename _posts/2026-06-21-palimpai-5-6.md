## Studies

### Articles

#### "Multispectral Imaging of the Archimedes Palimpsest"

This week, the first article I read, titled ["Multispectral Imaging of the Archimedes Palimpsest"](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/1284258), explains how MSI was used in the Archimedes Palimpsest Project. The project aims to uncover texts allegedly authored by Archimedes that were erased from the manuscripts and turned into a prayer book later in the thirteenth century.

The most interesting part of this article is the method used to partially uncover these texts: the authors turned the problem of uncovering the Archimedes text into a Least Squares problem. To achieve this, they assume each pixel of the multispectral image is a linear combination of multiple components, including the underlying text (the target) and the overlying text (the prayer book).

The article also tries out some other technique to achieve the separation and later combines the results in visible images.

While I'd never read any article that formalized the problem of uncovering palimpsests into a Least Squares problem, I interpret it as a subset of a segmentation problem: the idea that both texts are somewhat separable and that the observed image is a combination of components that include the underlying text, the overlying text, the paper and some noise.

#### "A deep learning experiment for semantic segmentation of overlapping characters in palimpsests"

Titled ["A deep learning experiment for semantic segmentation of overlapping characters in palimpsests"](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.01130), the article presents an approach to dealing with overlapping characters in palimpsests. 

The idea is to use CNNs (Convolutional Neural Networks) to examine images of overlapping characters and identify the original characters. But, as CNNs typically require large amounts of labeled data and the authors did not dispose of one large enough dataset, they opted to create a synthetic dataset to train a model as a proof of concept. The rest of the article explains the constraints of the synthetic dataset and training results.

While identifying the characters of the underlying text is another major challenge, I initially viewed it as an optional post-processing task. That is, even if we had an algorithm to identify these characters, we would not be able to apply it until the underlying text is uncovered, so the main problem still holds.

But after reflecting more on it, I realized that a more complex version of this model could be used to help identify the underlying text: rather than separating underlying and overlying text before character recognition, a similar model could be used to recover useful information even when both texts overlap.

Therefore, despite the apparent simplicity of the model proposed in this article, I found it valuable because it challenged some of my previous assumptions about the right moment character recognition should be applied in a pipeline. It also suggested a possible direction for future experiments.

## Experiments

### Revisiting PCA

In our last post, I described an experiment where naive PCA (without any form of pre-processing) was applied to a multispectral subject image to test if the underlying text would become more visible.

While the results were not impressive, they certainly weren't unexpected. As discussed in the post, all articles I read that use PCA as a main step do heavy pre and post-processing to the images. Therefore, simply applying PCA without any of the extra steps would most likely not yield such good results.

This week, I decided to change one important variable and re-do the experiment. In our previous post, I used PCA to reduce the number of channels of the multispectral image from 16 to 3. Then, by interpreting these new channels as RGB values, the result image was:

![naive PCA](/assets/img/posts/week4/pca_3_bands.png)

Now, I decided to reduce the number of channels from 16 to 1. By interpreting the results as a grayscale image, we get:

![grayscale PCA](/assets/img/posts/week5/pca_1_bands.png)

Observe that the underlying text is not necessarily more visible in the grayscale image. Instead, the main difference between the two results is that they appear to emphasize different components of the original manuscript.

If we zoom on the bottom right, you may see a coat of arms that resembles a horse in the grayscale image, while it is mostly invisible in the colored one. This can also be seen in the center of the image, where you can clearly see characters that may have been written on the reverse side of the paper.

![horse bottom right](/assets/img/posts/week5/combined_bottom-right.png)

Now if we look at the center of the colored image, we can see some cracks on the paper that are way less visible in the grayscale one. It also captured the dust and mold on the paper much more clearly, suggesting it might be the better at capturing the texture of the paper. 

![cracks center](/assets/img/posts/week5/combined_crop-center.png)

While all of these details are somewhat visible in the RGB reconstruction, changing the number of principal components retained by PCA appears to affect which features of the manuscript are emphasized. Some configurations make the texture of the paper more apparent, while others highlight subtle markings and symbols that are otherwise difficult to notice.
